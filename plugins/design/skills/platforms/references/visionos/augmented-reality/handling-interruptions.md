# Handling interruptions
ARKit loses track of device position and orientation during interruptions (like switching apps or taking a call). When this happens, virtual objects placed previously may appear in incorrect real-world locations upon return. If you implement relocalization support, ARKit uses new data to attempt to restore these virtual objects to their original positions. For detailed guidance, refer to [Managing Session Life Cycle and Tracking Quality](apple:ARKit/managing-session-life-cycle-and-tracking-quality).

- **Consider utilizing the system's coaching view to aid relocalization.** During relocalization, ARKit attempts to match its prior state with new environmental observations. The coaching view assists in making these observations more valuable by helping users return the device to its previous position and orientation.
- **Consider temporarily hiding previously placed virtual objects during relocalization.** To prevent visual artifacts like flickering during the relocalization process, it is often best practice to conceal virtual objects and then reappear them once their correct positions are established.
- **Minimize interruptions if your application supports both AR and non-AR modes.** One strategy to reduce interruptions is embedding the non-AR experience within the AR flow, allowing users to complete tasks without needing to exit and reenter the AR session. For instance, if your app allows users to select furniture by placing it in a room, you could allow them to change the upholstery without breaking the AR experience.
- **Allow users to cancel relocalization.** If users do not position and orient their device near its previous location before an interruption, relocalization may continue unsuccessfully indefinitely. If coaching the user to resume the session proves unsuccessful, consider offering a reset button or another mechanism to restart the AR experience.
- **Indicate when the front-facing camera fails to track a face for more than about half a second.** Use a visual cue to signal that the camera can no longer maintain tracking of the person's face. If textual instructions are necessary in this scenario, keep them extremely brief.
