# Activity views — full guidelines
You have the ability to supply app-specific activities that appear in a share sheet when users open it within your application or game. For instance, Photos offers app-specific actions like Adjust Location, Copy Photo, and Add to Album. By default, the system lists app-specific actions before general actions—such as those found in Add to Files or AirPlay—that are available across multiple apps or throughout the system. Users can modify this list of actions to ensure their most-used options are displayed and to include new ones.

You can also develop app extensions to offer custom share and action activities that users can utilize in other applications. (An *app extension* is code you provide for others to install and use outside of your app.) For example, you might build a custom share activity that users can install to assist them in sharing a webpage using a particular social media service. Although macOS does not natively provide an activity view, you can still create share and action app extensions for use on a Mac. For guidance, refer to [Share and action extensions](#Share-and-action-extensions).

### Best practices
- **Avoid creating duplicate versions of common actions already available in the activity view.** Supplying a redundant action, such as another Print option, is confusing and unnecessary since users won't know how to differentiate it from the system-provided action. If your app offers functionality similar to an existing action, assign it a unique title. For instance, if you allow users to print a bank transaction using custom formatting, use a title like “Print Transaction” so users understand the activity's purpose.
- **Consider using a symbol to represent your custom activity.** **SF Symbols** offers a wide range of configurable symbols that can be used to convey concepts and items within an activity view. If you must create a custom interface icon, ensure it is centered within an area of approximately 70x70 pixels. Refer to **Icons** for guidance.
- **Write a succinct, descriptive title for each custom action you provide.** If a title exceeds the available space, the system may truncate or wrap it. It is best to use a single verb or a brief verb phrase that clearly explains the action's function. Do not include your company or product name in an action title. Conversely, the Share Sheet displays the share activity's title—which is typically a company name—beneath its corresponding icon.
- **Make sure activities are appropriate for the current context.** While you cannot change the order of system-provided tasks in an activity view, you retain the ability to exclude tasks irrelevant to your application. For example, if printing is not a relevant function within your app, you can omit the Print activity. You also have control over which custom tasks are displayed at any given time.
- **Use the Share button to display an activity view.** Users expect that system-provided activities are accessed via the Share button. Do not confuse users by offering an alternative method to invoke this functionality.

### Share and action extensions
Share extensions offer users a simple way to share information from the current context with applications, social media platforms, and other services. Action extensions allow users to initiate content-specific tasks—such as bookmarking, copying a link, editing an inline image, or translating selected text—without leaving their current environment.

The system presents share and action extensions differently based on the platform:

- In iOS and iPadOS, users encounter share and action extensions within the share sheet that appears when they select an Action button.
- In macOS, users access share extensions by clicking a Share button in the toolbar or selecting Share from a context menu. An action extension can be accessed by hovering over specific embedded content types—like an image added to a Mail composition window—clicking a toolbar button, or choosing a quick action in Finder.
- **If necessary, create a custom interface that feels familiar to users.** For a share extension, prioritize the system-provided composition view because it offers a consistent sharing experience that users are already accustomed to. For an action extension, include your application's name. If you must present a custom interface, incorporate elements from your app’s UI to help users understand the connection between your extension and your main application.
- **Streamline and limit interaction.** Users appreciate extensions that allow them to complete a task in minimal steps. For instance, a share extension might instantly post an image to a social media account with just one tap or click.
- **Avoid placing a modal view above your extension.** By default, the system displays an extension within a modal view. Although displaying an alert above an extension might sometimes be required, refrain from introducing additional modal views.
- **If necessary, provide an image that communicates the purpose of your extension.** A share extension automatically utilizes your app icon, which helps assure users that your application provided the extension. For an action extension, it is preferable to use a **symbol** or design an interface **icon** that clearly indicates the task at hand.
- **Use your main app to denote the progress of a lengthy operation.** An activity view is dismissed immediately once users finish the task within your share or action extension. If a task requires significant time, continue it in the background and provide users with a way to check its status within your main application. While you may use a notification regarding an issue, do not notify them solely because the task has finished.
