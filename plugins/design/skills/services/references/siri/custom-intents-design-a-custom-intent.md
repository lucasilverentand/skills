# Design a custom intent
If a built-in SiriKit intent accurately reflects your action's purpose, utilize that existing intent rather than defining a custom one. For instance, if you are providing a shortcut to send a message, adopt [INSendMessageIntent](apple:Intents/INSendMessageIntent); similarly, if the shortcut is for playing media, adopt [INPlayMediaIntent](apple:Intents/INPlayMediaIntent). Refer to [System intents](#System-intents) for further guidance.

**If your app's action necessitates a custom intent, select the category that most closely aligns with the action.** A category informs the system about the general function of an intent or shortcut—such as search, download, or order—and influences the text and spoken dialogue presented to users when a shortcut is offered by the system or used with Siri. Since you are responsible for designing the conversational flow for any custom intents you offer, choosing a category that accurately represents each intent's meaning is crucial.

> **Tip**
> If the primary function of your action is to retrieve information or display content (like a sports score or current weather), use the information category. Selecting a different category will require users to take extra taps to obtain the desired information.

- **Design custom intents that expedite common, valuable tasks.** Leverage users' familiarity with your application by making it simpler for them to initiate the actions they perform most frequently.
- **Ensure that your intent functions reliably in all scenarios.** Make it effortless for users to execute your intent as a shortcut, regardless of how they initiate it. For example, be prepared for execution via voice on devices with or without a screen, from suggestions on the lock screen or Siri face on Apple Watch, through search, and within a multi-step shortcut.
- **In general, design custom intents for tasks that are not excessively complicated.** Users benefit most from intents that minimize the steps needed to complete a task. Do not undermine this simplicity by requiring users to engage in an extended conversation within your app. You can also minimize user errors by limiting custom intents to clearly defined tasks.
- **Design your intents to be enduring.** Avoid offering intents that are date-specific or tied to temporary data. For example, it is inadvisable for a travel application to offer a custom intent for each specific itinerary. A superior approach might involve using follow-up questions to allow users to retrieve an upcoming trip's itinerary.
- **Do not require permission to use Siri.** If your app only supports custom intents—and not system intents—you are not required to obtain permission to use Siri before allowing users to create and utilize voice shortcuts for your intents. Requesting permission can slow down the user experience and potentially discourage adoption of your app's custom intents.
- **Support background operation.** The most effective intents support shortcuts that execute rapidly and do not pull the user out of their current context. Aim to support custom intents that can run in the background without bringing your app into the foreground. Supporting background operation also guarantees users can complete the task using only voice and hands-free methods.
