# Enhance the voice experience for custom intents
- **Focus on natural, fluid dialogue.** You have control over all aspects of Siri's spoken responses, including how follow-up queries are handled. Developing a script and role-playing the exchange with another individual is recommended to assess how well your dialogue translates into a real-world conversation. This testing helps identify areas where the interaction feels unnatural.
- **Ensure users clearly understand errors and failures.** While the system provides default error messages, it is crucial to refine these responses so they are highly specific to the current situation. For instance, if a soup app is out of chicken noodle soup, a custom error like, “Sorry, we’re out of chicken noodle soup,” is superior to the generic response, “Sorry, we can’t complete your order.”
- **Develop highly engaging voice responses.** Remember that users may interact with your app via HomePod, using AirPods with “Hey Siri,” or through CarPlay without viewing a screen. In these scenarios, the voice output must convey the same essential information as the visual display to guarantee users receive necessary information regardless of their interaction method.
- **Craft voice responses that are concise, descriptive, and effective in hands-free use.** Similar to a shortcut title, an efficient custom spoken response must immediately communicate the current status of the shortcut execution. If follow-up questions are required, customize the default dialogue for maximum clarity. For example, “Which soup?” is clearer than “Which one?”
- **Prevent unnecessary repetition.** Since users may execute voice shortcuts frequently, they might hear the same prompt multiple times when addressing errors or providing follow-up answers. Leverage the current conversation context to minimize details in prompts. Avoid including superfluous words or attempts at humor, as both can become irritating over time.
- **Facilitate natural conversations with Siri.** Users interact with Siri in diverse ways; they might select a list item by saying “the second one,” or, in the context of a soup ordering app, they might say “large” or “small” instead of “bowl” or “cup.” You can make Siri interactions feel more intuitive by providing the system with alternative terms and phrases that function as app-specific synonyms (e.g., using “bowl” as a synonym for “large”). Also, enhance clarity by offering alternative dialogue options for Siri to speak. For example, the soup app might display onscreen menu items like “1 clam chowder,” or “1 clam chowder and 1 tomato,” but verbally present these options as, “Which order? The one with clam chowder only or the one that includes tomato?”
- **Omit your app name.** The system handles verbal and visual attribution for your application when responding to users. Including the app’s name in a spoken response is redundant and can detract from a natural experience. Furthermore, Siri speaks your app’s name less often after repeated shortcut usage because it is not needed to continually remind the user which app is responding.
- **Do not attempt to mimic or control Siri.** Never impersonate Siri, try to reproduce functionality provided by Siri, or deliver a response that appears to originate from Apple.
- **Maintain appropriateness and respect parental controls.** Never present content that is offensive or demeaning. Keep in mind that many households utilize parental controls to restrict explicit material and content based on specific rating levels.
- **Avoid using personal pronouns.** Develop content that is inclusive of all individuals.
- **Consider providing pathways for users to view more options within your app.** If the available list does not contain all necessary items, include an option that allows users to open your app for a complete view. In the list, you could use copy such as, “See more in *App Name*,” and in spoken dialogue, you might prompt users to say, “More options.”
- **Keep responses agnostic of the device.** Users may interact with your app via Apple Watch, HomePod, iPad, iPhone, or CarPlay. If device-specific wording is required, ensure it accurately reflects the user’s current hardware.
- **Do not advertise.** Do not include advertisements, marketing pitches, or in-app purchase sales into your intent content.
