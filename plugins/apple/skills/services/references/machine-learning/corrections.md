# Corrections
- **Give users intuitive and straightforward ways to make adjustments.** If your application makes an error, you must ensure that users are not confused about how to correct it. You can prevent confusion by displaying the steps your application took while performing an automated task. For instance, Photos displays the controls used for auto-cropping a picture, allowing users to refine or reverse those automatic results using the same controls.
- **Provide immediate value when a correction is made.** Acknowledge and reward user effort by instantly showing the corrected content, particularly when the feature is vital or responding to direct user input. Additionally, ensure that these updates are persisted so users do not need to repeat the same corrections.
- **Allow users to correct their previous corrections.** Occasionally, a user makes an adjustment without realizing it is incorrect. As with all corrections, respond immediately to subsequent updates and persist those changes.
- **Always weigh the benefits of a feature against the effort required to correct it.** Users may tolerate an error made by an automated feature, but they are likely to abandon the feature if it becomes easier for them to perform the task manually.
- **Never rely on corrections to compensate for poor initial results.** While corrections can mitigate the impact of a mistake, depending on them may erode user trust in your application and diminish the feature's overall value.
- **Learn from corrections when appropriate.** A correction serves as a form of [implicit](#Implicit-feedback) feedback that can provide valuable insight into where your application falls short of user expectations. Before using a correction to update your models, confirm that the adjustment will genuinely lead to higher quality outcomes.
- **When possible, favor guided corrections over freeform corrections.** Guided corrections propose specific alternatives, thus requiring less effort from the user; conversely, freeform corrections do not suggest specific options and require more input from the user. An example of a guided correction is speech-to-text providing users with a list of suggested text completions to choose from. In contrast, Photos allows freeform correction so users can adjust the auto-cropping of a photo as they wish. If applicable to your application, you may support a combination of guided and freeform corrections.
