# Playing audio — full guidelines
- **Silence.** Users enable silent mode to prevent unwanted interruptions from unexpected sounds, such as incoming message tones or ringtones. In this state, they also expect nonessential audio—like sound effects, keyboard clicks, or game soundtracks—to be muted. When silent mode is active, the device only reproduces audio that users explicitly start, such as media playback, alarms, or audio/video messages.
- **Volume.** Users expect that their volume adjustments will influence all system sounds—including music and in-app sound effects—regardless of the method used to change the volume. An exception exists for the iPhone's ringer volume, which can be managed independently in Settings.
- **Headphones.** Users employ headphones for private listening and, occasionally, hands-free operation. When connecting headphones, users expect sound to seamlessly reroute without interruption; conversely, upon disconnecting, they anticipate playback will pause instantly.

### Best practices
- **Adjust levels automatically when necessary — do not adjust the overall volume.** While your application can manage relative, independent audio levels to achieve a balanced mix, the operating system volume always dictates the final output.
- **Permit audio rerouting when feasible.** Users frequently wish to select a different audio output device (e.g., switching from speakers to a stereo, car radio, or Apple TV). Support this functionality unless there is a strong technical reason against it.
- **Utilize the system-provided volume view for audio adjustments.** The volume view offers both a volume slider and controls for output device selection. You have the option to customize the appearance of this slider. For detailed developer instructions, consult [MPVolumeView](apple:MediaPlayer/MPVolumeView).
- **Select an audio category appropriate for your app or game's sound usage.** The chosen audio category determines how your application's sounds interact with other audio, whether they continue playing in the background, or if they cease when the device is set to Silent/Ring mode. Whenever possible, select a category that aligns with user expectations for your app. For instance, do not interrupt music from another application if it is unnecessary. See [AVAudioSession.Category](apple:AVFAudio/AVAudioSession/Category-swift.struct) for developer guidance.

|Category|Meaning|Behavior|
|---|---|---|
|Solo ambient|Sound is not critical, but it silences other audio. For example, a game soundtrack.|Responds to the silence switch. Does not mix with other sounds. Does not play in the background.|
|Ambient|Sound is not critical, and it does not silence other audio. For example, a game that allows users to listen to music from another app during gameplay instead of the game's soundtrack.|Responds to the silence switch. Mixes with other sounds. Does not play in the background.|
|Playback|Sound is essential and may mix with other audio. For example, an audiobook or educational app that teaches a foreign language, which users might wish to continue listening to after exiting the application.|Does not respond to the silence switch. May or may not mix with other sounds. Can play in the background.|
|Record|Sound is being recorded. For example, a note-taking app offering an audio recording mode. An application of this nature might switch to the playback category if it allows users to listen back on recorded notes.|Does not respond to the silence switch. Does not mix with other sounds. Can record in the background.|
|Play and record|Sound is simultaneously recorded and played back. For example, an audio messaging or video calling application.|Does not respond to the silence switch. May or may not mix with other sounds. Can record and play in the background.|

- **Respond to audio controls only when it is contextually appropriate.** Users can control audio playback outside of your app's interface—such as via Control Center or headphone controls—regardless of whether your app is active or backgrounded. It is acceptable to respond to audio controls if your application is actively playing audio, operating in a clear audio context, or connected to a device utilizing Bluetooth or AirPlay. Otherwise, when a user activates a control, ensure it does not interrupt audio currently playing from another application.
- **Avoid repurposing audio controls.** Since users expect consistent behavior across all applications, it is crucial that your app does not redefine the function of an audio control. If your application does not support specific controls, it should ignore them.
- **Consider custom audio player controls only if you need to offer commands unsupported by the system.** For instance, you might wish to define custom increments for skipping forward or backward, or display content related to the playing audio, such as a sports score.
- **Inform other applications when your app finishes playing temporary audio.** If your application temporarily interrupts the audio of other apps, ensure your audio session is flagged in a manner that allows other applications to know when they can resume. See [notifyOthersOnDeactivation](apple:AVFAudio/AVAudioSession/SetActiveOptions/notifyOthersOnDeactivation) for developer guidance.

### Handling interruptions
While most applications and games utilize the system's default interruption handling, you have the ability to tailor this behavior to suit specific requirements.

- **Determine how to respond to audio-session interruptions.** For instance, if your application involves recording or other audio functions that users wish to maintain uninterrupted, you can configure the system to prevent an incoming call from interrupting the currently playing audio unless the user accepts it. Consider a VoIP application: when users close the iPad's Smart Folio while using the built-in microphone, the app must terminate the call. Closing the folio automatically mutes the microphone and, by default, interrupts the associated audio session. If a VoIP app restarts the audio session upon reopening the Smart Folio, it risks infringing on user privacy by unmuting the microphone without their awareness. You can examine an audio-session interruption to guide your response; consult [Handling audio interruptions](apple:AVFAudio/handling-audio-interruptions) for developer guidance.
- **When an interruption ends, determine whether to resume audio playback automatically.** Audio from another application may interrupt the sound your app is producing. An interruption can be *resumable*, such as an incoming telephone call, or *nonresumable*, like when a user begins a new music playlist. You must use the interruption type and your app's nature to decide whether playback should restart automatically. For example, a media player that is actively playing audio when interrupted must confirm the interruption type is resumable before continuing playback. Conversely, a game does not need to verify the interruption type before automatically resuming audio, as games often play sound without requiring explicit user initiation. Refer to [shouldResume](apple:AVFAudio/AVAudioSession/InterruptionOptions/shouldResume) for developer guidance.

## Platform guidance — iOS & iPadOS
**Utilize the system’s sound services to generate brief sounds and vibrations.** Developers seeking implementation details should refer to [Audio Services](apple:AudioToolbox/audio-services).
