# Layer design
While a flattened image can be used for your icon, utilizing layers provides the maximum control over how your icon design is rendered. A layered app icon conveys a sense of depth and vibrancy. On every platform, the system applies visual effects that react to environmental conditions and user interaction.

iOS, iPadOS, macOS, and watchOS app icons consist of a background layer and one or more foreground layers that combine to establish dimensionality. These icons acquire Liquid Glass characteristics, such as translucency, specular highlights, and frostiness, which respond to changes in lighting and, on iOS and iPadOS, device movement.

tvOS app icons employ between two and five layers to generate dynamism as they come into focus. When focused, the app icon advances to the foreground in response to remote control input, gently swaying while its surface illuminates. The use of transparency and the separation between layers creates a feeling of depth during the parallax effect.

A visionOS app icon includes a background layer and one or two layers placed atop it, forming a three-dimensional object that subtly expands when viewed. The system enhances the icon's visual depth by adding shadows that convey separation between layers and by utilizing the alpha channel of the upper layers to achieve an embossed appearance.

You use your preferred design software to create the individual foreground layers of your app icon. For iOS, iPadOS, macOS, and watchOS icons, you then import these layers into Icon Composer, a design utility included with Xcode and accessible from the [Apple Developer website](https://developer.apple.com/icon-composer). Within Icon Composer, you define the background layer, adjust foreground layer placement, apply visual effects like transparency, specify default, dark, clear, and tinted appearance variants, and export the icon for Xcode use. For further assistance, refer to [Creating your app icon using Icon Composer](apple:Xcode/creating-your-app-icon-using-icon-composer).

For tvOS and visionOS app icons, you add your icon layers directly to an image stack in Xcode to complete the icon. For developer guidance, see [Configuring your app icon using an asset catalog](apple:Xcode/configuring-your-app-icon).

- **Use clearly defined edges in foreground layers.** To ensure that system-rendered highlights and shadows appear optimal, avoid soft or feathered edges on foreground layer shapes.
- **Vary opacity in foreground layers to enhance depth and liveliness.** For instance, the Photos icon divides its centerpiece into multiple translucent layers, adding greater dynamism to the design. Importing fully opaque layers and then adjusting transparency in Icon Composer allows you to preview and modify your design based on how transparency interacts with system effects.
- **Design a background that both stands out and emphasizes foreground content.** Subtle gradients, transitioning from top to bottom or light to dark, tend to interact well with system lighting effects. Icon Composer supports both solid colors and gradients for background layers, often making it unnecessary to import custom background images. If you do include a background layer, ensure it is full-bleed and opaque.
- **Prefer vector graphics when bringing layers into Icon Composer.** Unlike raster images, vector formats (such as SVG or PDF) scale cleanly and maintain sharpness at any size. Outline artwork and convert text to outlines in your design. For mesh gradients and raster artwork, use the PNG format due to its lossless compression.
