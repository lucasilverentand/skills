# Charting data — full guidelines
A chart can serve different roles, from a straightforward graphic offering quick insights to a sophisticated, interactive feature that becomes central to your application and encourages users to explore data from diverse viewpoints. Regardless of complexity, charts can assist users in performing critical data-driven tasks, such as:

- Analyzing trends using historical or projected values.
- Illustrating the present status of a process, system, or quantity that evolves over time.
- Comparing different items—or the same item across different points in time—by contrasting data across multiple categories.

Not every dataset requires visualization via a chart. If your goal is merely to present data without needing to convey insights about it or assist in its analysis, consider presenting the information differently, perhaps using a list or table that users can sort, search, and scroll through.

### Best practices
- **Use a chart when you want to highlight important information about a dataset.** Since charts are highly visible, they naturally capture user attention. Leverage this prominence by ensuring that the information users gain from the data is communicated clearly.
- **Keep a chart simple, letting people choose when they want additional details.** Avoid the urge to include every possible data point in a single chart. Excessive data can overwhelm the visual presentation, making it difficult to discern relationships or intended information. If you must present a large volume of data or offer extensive functionality, provide users with mechanisms to reveal complexity gradually. For instance, allow users to select different levels of detail or specific data subsets based on their interest. To assist users in understanding how to operate an interactive chart, you might offer several versions, each offering progressively more functionality.
- **Make every chart in your app accessible.** A chart conveys information through both graphical representations and accompanying visual descriptions. Beyond the displayed visuals, it is essential to include accessibility labels that describe both the chart's components and its values, as well as accessibility elements that enable user interaction with the chart. For detailed guidance, refer to **Enhancing the accessibility of a chart**.

### Designing effective charts
- **Generally, favor standard chart types.** Users are typically familiar with common visualizations like bar and line charts; therefore, employing these established formats increases the likelihood that users will immediately understand your chart. For guidance on this practice, refer to **Charts**.
- **If a novel visualization is required, assist users in interpreting the chart.** For instance, when a Watch pairs with an iPhone, Activity introduces the Activity rings by animating them individually, demonstrating how each ring corresponds to the move, exercise, and stand metrics.
- **Analyze data from different levels or viewpoints to identify displayable details that enhance the chart.** For example, viewing data at a macro level assists in determining high-level summaries (such as totals or averages) that users may find relevant. A mid-level perspective can reveal ways to help users identify useful data subsets, while examining individual data points aids in drawing attention to specific values or items. Presenting information that allows users to view the chart from multiple angles encourages deeper engagement.
- **Enhance comprehension by including descriptive text within the chart.** Descriptive titles, subtitles, and annotations help emphasize the most critical information and highlight actionable conclusions within a chart. You may also display brief descriptive text that functions as a headline or summary, allowing users to grasp the essential information at a glance. For example, Weather displays text summarizing the immediate need—such as “Chance of light rain in the next hour”—above the scrolling list of hourly forecasts for the following 24 hours. While a descriptive headline or summary improves accessibility, it does not replace dedicated accessibility labels.
- **Match the chart's size to its function, topic, and level of detail.** Generally, a chart must be large enough to comfortably display all necessary details and expansive enough to support intended interactivity. For example, while it is always crucial that users can easily read a chart's details and descriptive text (like labels and annotations), you may also need sufficient space to allow users to modify the chart's scope or investigate data from different perspectives. Conversely, a smaller chart may be appropriate for offering glanceable information about a single item or providing a preview/snapshot of a larger version view.
- **Prefer consistency across multiple charts, only varying when differences must be emphasized.** If several charts in your application serve a similar function, you should generally avoid implying they are unrelated by using different styles or types. Furthermore, maintaining a consistent visual approach allows users to apply knowledge gained from one chart to understand another. Consider using different chart types and styles when the goal is to highlight meaningful distinctions between charts.
- **Maintain continuity among multiple charts utilizing the same data.** When using several charts to allow users to explore a single dataset from different angles, it is vital to use one chart type and maintain consistent colors, annotations, layouts, and descriptive text to signal that the underlying dataset is identical. For instance, the Health Trends screen displays small charts, each using a specific visual style to depict a recent trend (e.g., steps or resting heart rate). When users select the chart to reveal all their data in that area, the expanded version employs the same style, colors, marks, and annotations to reinforce the relationship between the versions.
