# Organization
Organizing menu items to reflect how users interact with your application or game contributes to a feeling of simplicity and intuitiveness.

- **Prioritize placing the most important or frequently accessed menu items first.** Since users typically begin scanning a menu from the top, leading with high-priority options allows them to locate desired items quickly without needing to read the entire list.
- **Group commands that are logically related.** For instance, grouping editing functions like Copy, Cut, and Paste, or camera controls such as Look Up, Look Down, and Look Left, aids users in remembering where to find them. To visually differentiate these groups, utilize a separator. Depending on the platform and menu type, this *separator* manifests as a horizontal line or a brief visual gap in the menu's background.
- **Maintain all logically connected commands within the same group, even if they vary in usage frequency.** For example, while Paste and Match Style might be used less often than Paste, users anticipate finding both commands alongside the more frequently accessed editing controls like Copy and Cut.
- **Pay attention to menu length.** Longer menus require more time and focus from the user, increasing the likelihood they might miss their desired command. If a menu becomes excessively long, consider breaking it into multiple menus. Alternatively, you may use a submenu to condense the list; for example, listing difficulty levels within a submenu accessed via a New Game menu item. The exception is menus containing user-generated or dynamically generated content, such as the History and Bookmarks menus in Safari. Users expect these types of menus to accommodate all added items, making a lengthy menu acceptable and scrolling necessary.
