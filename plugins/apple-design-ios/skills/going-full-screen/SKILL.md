# Going full screen
iPhone, iPad, and Mac devices support full-screen modes, allowing users to expand a window to occupy the entire display area. This feature conceals system controls and creates an environment free from distractions

## When to use
- User asks about **going full screen** on iOS and iPadOS (e.g. `"how do I design going full screen for iPhone"`).
- User is building an iPhone UI that needs going full screen and wants to follow Apple's guidelines.
- User asks to audit or review going full screen in an iOS and iPadOS design.
- User mentions going full screen in the context of an iPhone app, game, or interface.

Apple TV and Apple Watch do not feature full-screen modes because their respective apps and games already utilize the entire screen by default. Apple Vision Pro, similarly, does not offer a dedicated full-screen mode; users can instead expand a window to maximize their view or utilize the Digital Crown to conceal passthrough and transition into a more immersive experience (refer to [Immersive experiences](immersive-experiences.md) for guidance).

### Best practices
- **Support full-screen mode when it makes sense for your experience.** Full-screen mode is appreciated by users who wish to concentrate on a task or become immersed in content. Consider offering this mode if your experience involves playing games, viewing media (like videos or photo slideshows), or performing an in-depth task that benefits from a distraction-free setting.
- **If necessary, adjust your layout in full-screen mode, but don’t programmatically resize your window.** When the window is larger in full-screen mode than it is outside of it, you must keep crucial content visible while effectively utilizing the extra screen space. For instance, adjusting interface proportions without altering which elements are displayed might be appropriate. If you make such adjustments, ensure they are subtle enough to maintain a consistent interface and prevent visually jarring transitions between modes.
- **Continue to provide access to essential features and controls so people can complete their task without exiting full-screen mode.** For example, a media experience running in full screen must keep playback controls persistently available or easily accessible when needed.
- **Except in games, let people reveal the Dock while your iPadOS or macOS app is in full-screen mode.** In both iPadOS and macOS, it is vital to maintain accessibility to the Dock so users can quickly launch other applications or access Dock items. To prevent accidental revelation of the Dock while a user is playing your full-screen game, you may instruct iPadOS to ignore an initial swipe up from the screen’s bottom edge or hide the Dock entirely in macOS. For developer guidance, refer to [preferredScreenEdgesDeferringSystemGestures](apple:SwiftUI/UIHostingController/preferredScreenEdgesDeferringSystemGestures) (SwiftUI), [preferredScreenEdgesDeferringSystemGestures](apple:UIKit/UIViewController/preferredScreenEdgesDeferringSystemGestures) (UIKit), and [hideDock](apple:AppKit/NSApplication/PresentationOptions-swift.struct/hideDock) (AppKit).
- **After people switch away from your full-screen experience, help them resume where they left off when they return.** For example, a slideshow or game should automatically pause when the user leaves the experience to prevent loss of progress.
- **Let people choose when to exit full-screen mode.** Users generally do not anticipate that full-screen mode will automatically terminate when they switch to a different activity or complete an absorbing task, such as watching a movie or playing a game.
- **Prioritize content by temporarily hiding toolbars and navigation controls.** You can create a distraction-free environment by concealing elements when the content is the primary focus, such as during full-screen photo viewing or document reading. If you implement this behavior, allow users to restore the hidden elements using a familiar action or gesture, such as tapping, swiping down, or moving the cursor to the top of the screen. However, ensure controls remain visible when they are necessary for navigation or task completion. Although a visionOS window can hide its toolbars or navigation controls, users generally expect different types of immersive experiences while using Apple Vision Pro; for guidance, see [Immersive experiences](immersive-experiences.md).

## Platform guidance — iOS & iPadOS
**Consider delaying system gestures to prevent unintended exits when running in full-screen mode within an application or game.** By default, the Home Screen indicator automatically minimizes shortly after a user navigates to your application or game. It becomes visible again when the bottom portion of the screen is touched, enabling a single swipe to exit. Because this behavior aligns with user expectations and familiarity, it should be maintained whenever feasible. If implementing this feature leads to accidental exits, you have the option to require two swipes instead of one for exiting. For implementation guidance, consult [preferredScreenEdgesDeferringSystemGestures](apple:SwiftUI/UIHostingController/preferredScreenEdgesDeferringSystemGestures).
