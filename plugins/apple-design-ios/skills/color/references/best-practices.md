# Best practices
- **Avoid using the same color to signify multiple concepts.** Maintain strict color consistency throughout your interface, especially when colors are used to convey information such as status or interactivity. For instance, if your brand color indicates that a borderless button is actionable, employing the same or similar hue to style non-interactive text will cause confusion.
- **Ensure your app's colors render correctly across light, dark, and high-contrast modes.** iOS, iPadOS, macOS, and tvOS support both light and [Dark Mode](dark-mode.md) appearances. [System colors](#System-colors) subtly adjust based on the system appearance, ensuring proper color differentiation and contrast for text, symbols, and other components. When the Increase Contrast setting is active, these color differences become much more pronounced. Whenever feasible, utilize system colors, as they already incorporate variants for all these contexts. If you define a custom color, you must supply light and dark variants, along with an increased contrast option for each variant that offers significantly higher visual distinction. Even if your app is deployed in a single appearance mode, providing both light and dark color options supports Liquid Glass adaptivity.
- **Validate your app's color scheme under diverse lighting conditions.** The way colors appear varies depending on whether the app is viewed outdoors in sunlight or indoors in low light. In bright environments, colors appear darker and more subdued. Conversely, in dark settings, they appear brighter and highly saturated. In visionOS, colors may shift based on the physical surroundings—such as the color of a wall or object and how it reflects light. Adjust your app colors to guarantee an optimal viewing experience in most usage scenarios.
- **Test your application across different devices.** For example, the True Tone display (found on select iPhone, iPad, and Mac models) automatically uses ambient light sensors to adjust the display's white point according to the current environment. Apps primarily focused on reading, photography, video, or gaming can either enhance or diminish this effect by specifying a white point adaptivity style (see [UIWhitePointAdaptivityStyle](apple:BundleResources/Information-Property-List/UIWhitePointAdaptivityStyle) for developer guidance). When testing tvOS apps, use multiple brands of HD and 4K TVs with varied display settings. Furthermore, you can evaluate your app's appearance using different color profiles on a Mac—such as P3 and Standard RGB (sRGB)—by selecting a profile in System Settings > Displays. Refer to [Color management](#Color-management) for detailed guidance.
- **Consider the influence of artwork and translucency on adjacent colors.** Variations in artwork may necessitate adjustments to nearby colors to maintain visual coherence and prevent interface elements from appearing either overwhelming or insufficient. For instance, a map displays a light color scheme in map mode but transitions to a dark color scheme in satellite mode. Colors can also appear differently when applied to or placed behind a translucent element, such as a toolbar.
- **If your app allows users to select colors, prioritize system-provided color controls when available.** Utilizing native color pickers ensures a unified user experience and allows users to save palettes accessible from any application. For developer instructions, consult [ColorPicker](apple:SwiftUI/ColorPicker).
