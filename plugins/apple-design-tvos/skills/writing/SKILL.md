# Writing
The language selected within your application is fundamental to its user experience

## When to use
- User asks about **writing** on tvOS (e.g. `"how do I design writing for Apple TV"`).
- User is building an Apple TV UI that needs writing and wants to follow Apple's guidelines.
- User asks to audit or review writing in a tvOS design.
- User mentions writing in the context of an Apple TV app, game, or interface.

## Quick principles
- **Define your app's voice** — Consider your audience to determine the appropriate vocabulary
- **Align tone with context** — After establishing your app's voice, vary the tone according to the specific situation
- **Maintain clarity** — Select words that are easily understood and accurately convey the intended message
- **Write for all users** — To ensure maximum utility across diverse users, your app must communicate effectively with the broadest possible audience
- **Be action oriented** — Using active voice and explicit labels facilitates user navigation, whether moving between screens or progressing through steps within an app
- **Build language patterns** — Consistency fosters familiarity, contributing to an app that feels cohesive, intuitive, and thoughtfully designed
- **Adopt capitalization rules that align with your app’s style, then apply them consistently** — While specific components, such as [Content](buttons.md#Content), have defined guidelines, your text formatting reflects the app's overall voice
- **Give clear guidance and use consistent language throughout processes with multiple steps** — If a flow spans several screens, define how actions that transition users between steps will be labeled
- **Use possessive pronouns sparingly** — Possessive pronouns like *my* and *your* often add unnecessary context
- **Write for how people use each device** — Since users may interact with your app across different devices, while language consistency is required, consider where text adjustments would suit different…
- **Provide clear next steps on any blank screens** — An empty state—such as a completed to-do list or bookmarks folder with no items—offers an opportunity to welcome users and educate them…
- **Write clear error messages** — It is always preferable to help users prevent errors
