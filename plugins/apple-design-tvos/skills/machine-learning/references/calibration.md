# Calibration
Calibration is the process where users supply necessary information for an app feature to become functional. For instance, using Face ID requires the user to scan their face initially.

Generally, only implement calibration when your feature cannot operate without this initial data. If the feature can function independently of calibration, consider alternative methods to gather required information, such as [implicit](#Implicit-feedback) or possibly [explicit](#Explicit-feedback).

- **Always secure users’ information.** Since sensitive data may be provided during calibration, ensuring its security is mandatory.
- **Be transparent about why you need users’ information.** Because calibration often precedes feature usage, it is vital that users understand the value of providing their data. Briefly explain how your feature benefits them, focusing on its function rather than its inner workings.
- **Collect only the most essential information.** Designing a unique experience that requests minimal data enhances user comfort and increases trust in your application.
- **Do not require users to undergo calibration more than once.** Furthermore, it is optimal if calibration occurs early in the user journey. As users continue to interact with your app or feature, you can use implicit or explicit feedback to refine your understanding of them without requiring repeated calibration. An exception applies if the feature requires object-based calibration rather than person-based calibration; for example, an app assisting with baseball swings might need to calibrate with each new field.
- **Make calibration quick and effortless.** Even a brief calibration consumes user time and effort. An ideal calibration experience allows users to respond easily without sacrificing data quality. The following guidelines will assist in creating a streamlined calibration process:
- Prioritize acquiring critical data points and infer the remainder from other sources or user feedback.
- Avoid requesting information that most users would need to look up.
- Avoid requiring users to perform difficult actions.
- **Ensure users know how to successfully complete calibration.** Once users commit to the calibration process, provide them with a clear objective and visible progress indicators. For example, Face ID calibration briefly outlines the required actions and updates the tick mark visualization around the face as scanning progresses.
- **Provide immediate assistance if progress halts.** When progress stops, users may feel stuck or powerless, potentially eroding trust in your application. In such instances, it is crucial to offer actionable advice that quickly guides them back on track. While offering this guidance, never imply fault or error on the user's part, and always provide a clear next step.
- **Confirm successful completion.** The moment calibration is successfully finished, reward the user's time and effort by providing a clear path to using the feature. Explicitly confirming calibration completion reinforces the unique nature of the experience and helps users shift focus to your feature.
- **Allow users to cancel calibration at any time.** Ensure there is an easy mechanism for users to discontinue the experience at any point, without implying judgment regarding their choice. No specific messaging about the canceled calibration is necessary, as users will have another opportunity to participate the next time they attempt to use the feature.
- **Provide a way for users to update or delete information provided during calibration.** Allowing users to modify their input grants them greater control and fosters increased trust in your application. While the calibration experience may assist with edits, it is advisable to allow users to adjust their information outside of the calibration flow so they feel free to make changes whenever necessary.
