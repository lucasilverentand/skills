# Depth
Humans naturally use visual indicators such as distance, occlusion, and shadow to perceive depth and understand their environment. On Apple Vision Pro, the system automatically applies visual effects—including color temperature, reflections, and shadows—to assist users in perceiving the depth of virtual content. When a user moves a virtual object, or when they change their orientation relative to that object, these visual effects alter the object's perceived depth, resulting in a more realistic experience.

Since users may view your content from any angle, incorporating subtle depth throughout your interface—even within standard windows—can enhance its natural appearance. When developing with SwiftUI, the system applies visual effects to views contained within a 2D window, giving them an appearance of depth. For guidance on this process, refer to [Adding 3D content to your app](apple:visionOS/adding-3d-content-to-your-app).

If your content requires additional depth, you must utilize RealityKit to construct a 3D object (see [RealityKit](apple:RealityKit) for developer guidance). This 3D object can be displayed anywhere, or it can utilize a *volume*, which is a component dedicated to displaying 3D content. A volume functions similarly to a window, but without a visible frame. Consult [visionOS volumes](windows.md#visionOS-volumes) for details.

- **Ensure visual cues accurately convey your content's depth.** If these visual indicators are absent or contradict a user’s real-world experience, it can lead to visual discomfort.
- **Leverage depth to communicate hierarchy.** Depth assists an object in appearing distinct from surrounding content, thereby increasing its prominence. Users are also sensitive to changes in depth: for instance, when a sheet appears over a window, the window recedes along the z-axis, allowing the sheet to advance and gain visual focus.
- **Generally, avoid applying depth effects to text.** Text that appears suspended above its background is difficult to read, which slows down the user and may sometimes induce vision discomfort.
- **Ensure depth adds functional value.** Generally, you should employ depth to clarify and enhance the experience—it is not necessary everywhere. As you introduce depth into your design, consider the size and relative importance of objects. Depth is highly effective for visually separating large, critical elements in your application, such as making a tab bar or toolbar stand out from a window. However, it may be less effective on small objects. For example, using depth to make a button's symbol pop out from its background can reduce legibility and usability. Additionally, review the frequency with which you use varying depths throughout your application. Users must refocus their eyes to register each depth difference, and doing this too often or too rapidly can cause fatigue.
