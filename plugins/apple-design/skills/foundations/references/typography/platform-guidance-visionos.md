# Platform guidance — visionOS
SF Pro serves as the system font in visionOS. If you are using NY, you must specify the desired type styles.

visionOS employs bolder versions of Dynamic Type's body and title styles, and it introduces Extra Large Title 1 and Extra Large Title 2 for expansive, editorial layouts. For guidance on using vibrancy to establish hierarchy in text and symbols, refer to **visionOS**.

- **In general, prefer 2D text.** The greater the visual depth of a text character, the more challenging it can be to read. While limited 3D text may offer an engaging visual element, if the content requires comprehension, favor text with little or no visual depth. **Make sure text looks good and remains legible when people scale it.** Select a text style that appears optimal at full scale, then test its legibility across different scales.
- **Maximize the contrast between text and the background of its container.** By default, the system displays white text because this color provides strong contrast against the standard system background material, making it easier to read. Should you choose a different text color, ensure rigorous testing across diverse contexts.
- **If you need to display text that’s not on a background, consider making it bold to improve legibility.** In this situation, you should generally avoid adding shadows to increase contrast. The current space may not include a visual surface upon which to cast an accurate shadow, and predicting the size and density of a shadow that works well with a person’s current Environment is unpredictable.
- **Keep text facing people as much as possible.** If you display text associated with a specific point in space, such as a label for a 3D object, you should generally use *billboarding*—meaning the text must face the wearer regardless of movement. If you fail to rotate the text to maintain orientation, it may become impossible to read because viewers might view it from a side or highly oblique angle. For example, imagine a virtual lamp on a physical desk with an overhead label. To remain readable, the label must rotate around the y-axis as people move around the desk; in other words, the text baseline must stay perpendicular to the person’s line of sight.
