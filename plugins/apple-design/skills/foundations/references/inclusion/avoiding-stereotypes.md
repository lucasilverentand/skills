# Avoiding stereotypes
Everyone holds biases and stereotypes, often unconsciously, and recognizing how these affect one's thoughts can be difficult. A core objective of inclusive design is achieving self-awareness regarding your biases and generalizations so you can identify where they might influence your design choices.

For instance, consider an application designed to manage account access for different family members. If this app operates under a stereotypical definition of *family*—such as comprising a woman, a man, and their biological children—it is likely to convey this narrow viewpoint through its imagery and copy. Because the app presumes that users' families fit this limited definition, it inadvertently excludes anyone whose family structure differs.

Although the assumption made in the account-access app might seem obviously flawed, it is crucial to understand that not all assumptions are immediately apparent. Consider, for example, an app or game requiring users to select security questions for future identity verification:

- What was your favorite subject in college?
- What was the make of your first car?
- How did you feel when you first saw a rainbow?

From some viewpoints, these questions refer to common experiences, but all are rooted in backgrounds that not everyone shares. Using a context-specific experience to communicate something is ineffective for anyone lacking that shared context, thereby causing exclusion. To develop alternatives to the culture- and capability-specific questions listed above, you might reference more universal human experiences, such as:

- What’s your favorite activity?
- What was the name of your first friend?
- What quality describes you best?

Relying on stereotypes or assumptions in design decisions inevitably leads to exclusion because generalizations cannot account for the full spectrum of human perspectives. By avoiding assumptions and focusing instead on inclusion, you can create experiences that benefit all users.
