# Welcoming language
Using clear and inclusive language ensures that everyone feels welcome and understands your application or game. Thoroughly review all text in your experience to guarantee that your tone and word choices do not alienate any users. Below are several guidelines for creating copy—the text users read—that is direct, easy to grasp, and universally welcoming.

- **Consider the tone of your copy from different perspectives.** The manner in which you write conveys nearly as much meaning as the words themselves. While different applications employ different communication styles, ensure that your chosen tone does not unintentionally send unintended messages. For instance, an overly academic tone might suggest to users that the app or game is only intended for those with advanced education. As you refine the style appropriate for your experience, maintain clarity, directness, and respectfulness.
- **Pay attention to how you refer to people.** It is generally most effective to use *you* and *your* when addressing individuals directly. Referring to users indirectly, such as using *the user* or *the player*, can make the experience feel distant and unwelcoming. Furthermore, consider reserving terms like *we* and *our* to represent your software or company; otherwise, these pronouns might suggest a personal relationship with the user base that could be misinterpreted as condescending or insulting.
- **Avoid using specialized or technical terms without defining them.** While employing specialized or technical vocabulary can make your writing more concise, it also excludes individuals unfamiliar with those terms. If such terminology must be used, ensure you define it upfront and make the definitions readily accessible. Even if users are familiar with a term's definition within a sentence, the text is clearer—and easier to translate—when it employs plain language.
- **Replace colloquial expressions with plain language.** Colloquialisms are often tied to specific cultures and can prove challenging to translate. Moreover, some informal phrases carry exclusionary connotations that users might not be aware of. For example, the terms *peanut gallery* and *grandfathered in* both originated in contexts of oppression and continue to exclude groups. Even when a colloquial phrase lacks an exclusionary meaning, it can still alienate anyone who does not understand it.
- **Consider carefully before including humor.** Humor is highly subjective and, similar to colloquial expressions, it is difficult to translate across cultures. Incorporating humor into your experience risks confusing those who do not grasp it, irritating those who encounter it repeatedly, and offending those who interpret it differently. For additional guidance on writing, please consult [Writing inclusively](https://help.apple.com/applestyleguide/#/apdcb2a65d68).
