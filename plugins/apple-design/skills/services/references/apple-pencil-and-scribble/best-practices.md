# Best practices
- **Support behaviors users naturally expect from a marking instrument.** Most individuals are familiar with physical marking tools, and this background knowledge shapes their expectations when using Apple Pencil within your application. To deliver a highly satisfactory experience, consider how users interact with traditional pencils, pens, and other marking devices, and proactively support actions they might naturally attempt. For instance, users often wish to write in the margins of documents or books.
- **Allow users control over switching between Apple Pencil and finger input.** For example, if your app supports Apple Pencil for marking, ensure that the application's controls are also responsive to Apple Pencil input, preventing users from needing to switch back to finger use just to activate them. If a control does not support Apple Pencil input, it may appear unresponsive, suggesting a malfunction or low battery. (Scribble functionality supports only Apple Pencil input.)
- **Enable marking the instant Apple Pencil contacts the display.** The experience of applying Apple Pencil to the screen must mirror putting a classic pencil on paper; therefore, it is crucial that users are not required to tap a button or enter a specific mode before they can begin marking.
- **Enable self-expression by responding to how Apple Pencil is used.** Apple Pencil can detect tilt (altitude), force (pressure), orientation (azimuth), and [Barrel roll](#Barrel-roll). Utilize this data to influence the strokes Apple Pencil creates, such as by varying thickness and intensity. When responding to pressure, maintain simplicity and intuition. For example, affecting continuous properties—like ink opacity or brush size—by varying pressure feels natural.
- **Provide visual feedback confirming a direct link to the content.** Ensure that Apple Pencil appears to be immediately and directly manipulating the content it touches on screen. Avoid scenarios where Apple Pencil seems to initiate unrelated actions or affect parts of the screen distant from its contact point.
- **Optimize for both left- and right-handed usage.** Avoid placing controls in locations that may be blocked by either hand. If there is a possibility that controls might become obscured, consider allowing users to reposition them.
