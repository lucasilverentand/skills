# Touch controls
For iOS and iPadOS games, supporting touch interaction allows you to overlay virtual controls onto game content while simultaneously permitting players to interact directly with in-game elements. You can integrate these virtual controls using the [Touch Controller](apple:TouchController) framework. Adhere to the following guidelines to ensure an optimal touch control experience.

- **Assess whether virtual controls are necessary on top of game content.** Generally, virtual controls benefit games with numerous actions or those requiring movement control. However, sometimes direct interaction with in-game objects provides a more immersive and effective gameplay experience. Seek opportunities to minimize the overlap between virtual controls and game content by mapping actions to in-game gestures instead. For instance, consider allowing players to tap objects for selection rather than adding a dedicated virtual selection button.
- **Position virtual buttons in easily accessible locations.** Consider the device's physical boundaries and **Guides and safe areas**, along with comfortable control placements. Ensure buttons do not interfere with system components like the Home indicator or Dynamic Island on iPhone. Place frequently used controls near a player’s thumb, avoiding the circular areas typically reserved for camera and movement input. Place secondary controls, such as menus, toward the top of the screen.
- **Ensure controls are adequately sized.** Make sure frequently used controls maintain a minimum size of 44x44 pt, while less critical controls like menus require a minimum size of 28x28 pt to accommodate finger input.
- **Always include visible and tactile press states.** A virtual control feels unresponsive without clear visual and physical feedback regarding its activation state. Help players confirm successful interaction by adding a visible press state effect, such as a glow, that remains apparent while the control is being touched. Enhance this feedback by combining the press state with sound and haptics. Refer to **Playing haptics** for guidance.
- **Use symbols that clearly communicate the actions.** Select artwork that visually represents the function of each button (e.g., a weapon graphic for attack). Avoid abstract shapes or controller-based naming conventions like A, X, or R1, as these hinder player understanding and recall.
- **Dynamically show and hide virtual controls based on gameplay context.** Utilize the dynamic nature of touch controls to adjust which controls are visible depending on the current game state. Hiding controls when an action is unavailable or irrelevant reduces clutter and allows players to focus on critical elements. For example, consider keeping movement controls hidden until the player touches the screen.
- **Consolidate functionality into a single control.** Consider redesigning game mechanics that currently require simultaneous or sequential button presses. Leverage gestures like double tap and touch and hold to provide variations of a single action (e.g., using touch and hold for a powered-up attack). For actions involving multiple inputs, such as walking or sprinting, consider mapping them to a single control.
- **Map movement and camera controls to predictable behavior.** Players typically expect the left side of the screen to control movement and the right side to control camera direction. Maximize the playable area for both movement and camera input by using the largest possible input zones. For movement control, display a virtual thumbstick that reflects where the player’s thumb is placed, rather than maintaining a static position. For camera control, use direct touch panning instead of relying on a virtual thumbstick.
