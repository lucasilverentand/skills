# Best practices
- **Let people use the entire display.** Maximize the screen real estate dedicated to displaying both the physical world and your application's virtual elements. Avoid cluttering the view with controls or information that detract from immersion.
- **Strive for convincing illusions when placing realistic objects.** Design highly detailed 3D assets with lifelike textures so that objects appear genuinely integrated into the physical environment where they are placed. By utilizing ARKit data, you can correctly scale objects and position them on detected real-world surfaces, accurately reflecting environmental lighting conditions, simulating camera grain, casting top-down diffuse shadows onto real-world surfaces, and updating visuals as the camera moves. To help maintain the illusion you create, ensure your app updates scenes at 60 frames per second to prevent objects from appearing jumpy or flickering.
- **Consider how virtual objects with reflective surfaces show the environment.** Reflections in ARKit are approximations based on the camera-captured environment. To sustain the illusion that the AR experience is genuine, favor small or coarse reflective surfaces that minimize the impact of these approximations.
- **Use audio and haptics to enhance the immersive experience.** A sound effect or vibration is an effective way to confirm that a virtual object has made contact with a physical surface or another virtual object. Background music can also help immerse users in the virtual world. For guidance, refer to **Playing audio** and **Playing haptics**.
- **Minimize text in the environment.** Only display the information that is essential for your app experience.
- **If additional information or controls are necessary, consider displaying them in screen space.** Content displayed in *screen space* appears fixed to a consistent location, either within the virtual world or, less commonly, on the device screen. Content in screen space is usually easy for users to locate and view because it remains stationary while the underlying AR environment shifts with the device.
- **Consider using indirect controls when you need to provide persistent controls.** *Indirect controls* are not part of the virtual environment; rather, they are 2D controls presented in screen space. If your app requires persistent controls, consider placing them so users do not have to adjust how they hold the device to reach them. Also, consider using translucency in an indirect control to prevent it from blocking the underlying scene. For instance, the Measure app uses screen space with a mix of translucent and opaque controls for measuring real-world objects.
- **Anticipate that people will use your app in a wide variety of real-world environments.** Users may launch your app in locations with limited movement space or without large, flat surfaces. Clearly communicate your app's requirements and expectations upfront so users understand how their physical environment might influence the AR experience. You may also consider offering different feature sets for different environments.
- **Be mindful of people’s comfort.** Prolonged use of a device held at a specific distance or angle can cause fatigue. To help prevent discomfort, consider placing objects at a range that reduces the need for users to move the device closer; in games, consider keeping levels short and interspersed with brief breaks.
- **If your app encourages people to move, introduce motion gradually.** For example, you shouldn't require users to dodge a virtual projectile immediately upon entering your AR game. Allow users time to adapt to the AR experience in your app before progressively encouraging movement.
- **Be mindful of people’s safety.** When users are immersed in an AR experience, they may not be fully aware of their physical surroundings, so rapid, sweeping, or expansive motions could pose a danger. Consider ways to ensure your app is safe to operate; for example, a game could avoid encouraging large or sudden movements.
