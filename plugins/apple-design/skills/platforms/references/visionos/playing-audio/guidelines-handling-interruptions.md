# Handling interruptions
While most applications and games utilize the system's default interruption handling, you have the ability to tailor this behavior to suit specific requirements.

- **Determine how to respond to audio-session interruptions.** For instance, if your application involves recording or other audio functions that users wish to maintain uninterrupted, you can configure the system to prevent an incoming call from interrupting the currently playing audio unless the user accepts it. Consider a VoIP application: when users close the iPad's Smart Folio while using the built-in microphone, the app must terminate the call. Closing the folio automatically mutes the microphone and, by default, interrupts the associated audio session. If a VoIP app restarts the audio session upon reopening the Smart Folio, it risks infringing on user privacy by unmuting the microphone without their awareness. You can examine an audio-session interruption to guide your response; consult [Handling audio interruptions](apple:AVFAudio/handling-audio-interruptions) for developer guidance.
- **When an interruption ends, determine whether to resume audio playback automatically.** Audio from another application may interrupt the sound your app is producing. An interruption can be *resumable*, such as an incoming telephone call, or *nonresumable*, like when a user begins a new music playlist. You must use the interruption type and your app's nature to decide whether playback should restart automatically. For example, a media player that is actively playing audio when interrupted must confirm the interruption type is resumable before continuing playback. Conversely, a game does not need to verify the interruption type before automatically resuming audio, as games often play sound without requiring explicit user initiation. Refer to [shouldResume](apple:AVFAudio/AVAudioSession/InterruptionOptions/shouldResume) for developer guidance.

## Platform guidance — visionOS
Subtle, expressive sounds are integrated throughout visionOS to enhance experiences and provide crucial feedback when users interact with virtual objects using gestures. The system achieves *Spatial Audio* by combining audio algorithms with data about the user's physical environment, allowing sound to be perceived as originating from specific locations in space rather than just from speakers.

> **Important**
> In visionOS, as with any platform, never rely solely on sound to convey important information. Always include alternative methods to help users understand your application. For guidance, refer to **Accessibility**.

In visionOS, audio playback from the Now Playing app automatically pauses when its window is closed. Additionally, audio from any application other than Now Playing can duck when the user shifts focus to a different app.

- **Prefer playing sound.** Users generally expect sounds to remain audible while wearing the device, so an app that remains silent—especially during an immersive moment—may feel lifeless or appear broken. Throughout the design process, seek opportunities to introduce meaningful sounds that aid navigation and help users grasp the spatial qualities of your application.
- **Design custom sounds for custom UI elements.** Typically, system-provided components produce sound to assist users in locating them and receiving interaction feedback. To enable user interaction with your custom elements, design sounds that offer feedback and enhance the app's spatial experience.
- **Use Spatial Audio to create an intuitive, engaging experience.** Since users perceive Spatial Audio as originating from anywhere around them, it is particularly effective in fully immersive contexts for making an experience feel lifelike. *Ambient audio* provides pervasive sounds that can ground users in a virtual world, while an *audio source* simulates sound coming from a particular object. When building your app's soundscape, consider utilizing both types of audio.
- **Consider defining a range of places from which your app sounds can originate.** Spatial Audio assists users in pinpointing the object producing sound, whether that object is stationary or moving within the space. For instance, if users move an app window playing audio, the sound continues to originate directly from that window, regardless of its location.
- **Consider varying sounds that people could perceive as repetitive over time.** For example, the system subtly alters the pitch and volume of virtual keyboard sounds, mimicking the diverse noises a physical keyboard makes as users naturally vary typing speed and force. An efficient method to achieve pleasing sound variation is to randomize a sound file's pitch and volume during playback, rather than creating multiple distinct files.
- **Decide whether you need to play sound that’s fixed to the wearer or tracked by the wearer.** Users perceive *fixed* sound as if it is directed at them, irrespective of their viewing direction or the movement of virtual objects. Conversely, users tend to perceive *tracked* sound as originating from a specific object; therefore, moving that object closer or farther away alters what they hear. While tracked sound is generally preferred for enhancing realism, there are scenarios where fixed sound remains the appropriate choice. For example, Mindfulness utilizes fixed sound to envelop the wearer in a peaceful and engaging setting.
