# Best practices
- **Maximize drag and drop support throughout your application.** Most users are accustomed to drag and drop, and they frequently attempt to use it across many contexts. When you utilize standard system components—such as text fields and text views—drag and drop functionality is inherently supported.
- **Provide alternative methods for drag-and-drop actions.** Since drag-and-drop operations can sometimes be difficult or impossible for users to execute, it is crucial to offer alternative ways to achieve the same outcomes. For instance, you could implement menu commands allowing users to copy an item and relocate it elsewhere. In iOS and iPadOS, you can leverage accessibility APIs to designate sources and destinations, enabling users who rely on assistive technologies to perform drag-and-drop within your app (referencing [accessibilityDragSourceDescriptors](apple:ObjectiveC/NSObject-swift.class/accessibilityDragSourceDescriptors) and [accessibilityDropPointDescriptors](apple:ObjectiveC/NSObject-swift.class/accessibilityDropPointDescriptors) for developer guidance).
- **Define whether dragging and dropping content within your app constitutes a move or a copy.** Generally, a move is appropriate when the source and destination containers are identical—for example, moving text from one location to another within a document. A copy is appropriate when the containers differ, such as moving an image from one document to another. Before altering these defaults, consider the behavior that most users anticipate and prefer; choose the option least likely to cause frustration or data loss.
- **Support multi-item drag and drop when it is relevant.** Users value the efficiency of dragging a collection of items to a destination rather than handling each item individually. On iOS, iPadOS, macOS, and visionOS, users can select multiple items and drag them as a group. Furthermore, macOS allows users to select multiple items across different applications and drag them collectively. In iPadOS, a user can initiate the drag operation with one item and subsequently add others to the group without interrupting the action.
- **Favor allowing users to undo a drag-and-drop operation.** Occasionally, users unintentionally drop content in the incorrect location; therefore, they benefit from the ability to reverse the action and return to their previous state. You may also mitigate errors by prompting for confirmation before completing a drag-and-drop operation that cannot be undone. For example, the Finder on macOS prompts for confirmation when a file is dragged into a write-protected folder because the user may be unable to open that folder or remove the dropped item. In certain scenarios, providing a mechanism to reverse drag and drop results may be beneficial. For instance, Photos allows users to cancel photo sharing after dropping a photo into a shared stream.
- **Consider offering multiple fidelity versions of dragged content, ordered from highest to lowest.** By providing different alternatives, the destination application can select the most acceptable quality version. For example, if users drag a line drawing created in your app, you could offer options such as a PDF vector representation, a lossless PNG image with transparency, and a lossy JPEG image without transparency, in that specific sequence. Another scenario involves an application utilizing complex objects, such as charts; this app might provide the native chart object followed by a simpler version—like an image of the chart—for destinations that do not support chart objects.
- **Evaluate supporting spring loading.** Spring loading enables users to activate specific controls, such as buttons and segmented controls, by dragging selected content over them. For example, Calendar allows users to drag a selected event onto the day, week, month, or year segments in the toolbar, offering a convenient way to reschedule the event. On a Mac equipped with a Magic Trackpad, a button or segmented control can activate when the user force-clicks it while maintaining the content drag; on iPad, these components become active when hovered over while holding the content.
