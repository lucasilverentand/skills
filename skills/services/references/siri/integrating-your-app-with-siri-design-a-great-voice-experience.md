# Design a great voice experience
A superior voice interface builds user confidence that their requests will be fulfilled, even when they are unsure how to phrase them. Siri supports distinct voice experiences for both system-provided intents and custom intents. With a system intent, Siri manages the natural language processing, allowing users to interact with your application through different conversational flows. Conversely, a custom intent requires your app to enable users to perform an action that Siri is not natively aware of, resulting in a different kind of voice support. Custom intents offer extra opportunities to tailor the conversation flow but also require users to compose and speak a specific phrase to initiate the interaction.

As a designer, you have multiple avenues to improve both types of conversational experiences and assist users in specifying their needs without requiring extended dialogues.

For system-provided intents, you facilitate communication between Siri and the user regarding the desired action by supplying crucial information and defining any application-specific terminology that may arise during the conversation. You are not required to script additional dialogue for Siri because it already understands actions within system-defined domains and recognizes many ways users might describe them. For instance, if you need to confirm the recipient's name during a messaging intent's resolution phase, you simply indicate that a required parameter value is missing, and Siri prompts the sender with, “Who do you want to send it to?”

Despite not writing custom dialogue for system-provided intents, you can still enhance the voice experience in other ways. For example, if users instruct Siri to “play MyMusicApp” upon entering the gym, you could respond by playing their workout playlist.

When supporting a custom intent, users begin the action using their personal invocation phrase; if they do not use this phrase precisely, Siri will not activate the intent. While you can suggest a memorable phrase for users to employ, your primary responsibility is crafting clear, direct dialogue—often in the form of follow-up questions—to help users complete the task in the fewest steps possible.

For example, a coffee application might suggest *Order coffee* as a phrase users could use to reorder a favorite beverage. In a scenario where *Order coffee* is typically used to order different sizes of cappuccino, the coffee app could follow up with custom dialogue that builds on this context, such as “What size of cappuccino?” For other types of actions, more open-ended questions may be better suited for efficiently guiding users through the task. For example, if users begin a grocery shopping action by saying *Add to cart*, the grocery application could follow up with, “OK, what do you want?”
